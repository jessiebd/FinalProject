The Liars' Club

By Mary Karr

Beginnings

Arguably, the beginning of a book is important for catching the attention of readers. Not only does Karr acheive this in her shocking introduction, but the rhetorical moves that are set in motion by this intro are hugely important to the memoir as a whole.

Context

The Liars' Club is a story that discusses the complex family dynamics, set in the East Texas town of Leechfield. The memoir begins by citing Karr's most prominent memory from her childhood, where she is being asked by the family doctor to show [him] the marks. We as readers later learn that her mother has been taken away for being 'Nervous', and we can draw the conclusion that her mother is the cause of the doctor's concerns about Mary, although she makes it clear that she was not physically harmed by her mother in this occurrence.

Father Figures & Family

Karr basically idolizes her father the entirety of her childhood, a fact supported by the many instances in which Karr speaks fondly of him and his tenderness towards her.

With Daddy and his friends, I always knew what would happen and that left me feeling a sort of dreamy safety.

Karr's closeness with her father may be explained by the fact that her mother was not emotionally or physically all that present during her childhood. After the incident with the doctor, as mentioned above, her mother is institutionalized for a time. Additionally, while The Glass Castle does not really mention extended family members until about the halfway point, The Liars' Club tackles the complex and messy family dynamics that include Karr's grandmother. Karr sees how her own mother chafes under the pressure of her mother being around her family. Sadly, Karr's grandmother is also dying of cancer, the treatment of which addles her mind and further demonizes her in Karr's eyes. At the time, mustard gas was used to try and stop the spread of melanoma in the elderly woman's leg, but it ended up being amputated. After visiting her dying mother in the hospital, Karr's mother is emotionally absent, with Karr likening her to the dull-coated panther at the zoo. Karr quotes the German poet Rilke, who wrote;

It seems there are/ a thousand bars; and behind the bars, no world./ As he paces in cramped circles, over and over / the movement of his powerful soft strides/ is like a ritual dance around a center/ in which a might will stands paralyzed.

What I found most interesting as I have compared these two memoirs is that both Karr and Walls make mention of

With Mother, I always felt on edge of something new, something never before seen or read about or bought, something that would change us. When you climbed in the car with her, you never knew where you'd end up.
